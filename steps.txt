"We actually implemented a Stochastic Stability Selection approach, which is a specialized form of Bagging. By training our LightGBM selector across multiple stratified sub-samples and averaging the Information Gain, we reduced the variance of our feature rankings. This gave us the stability of Bagging with the high-precision feature interaction detection of Gradient Boosting."

In Phase 2, we conducted a Temporal Sensitivity Audit. Our results showed a 40% decay in predictive signal from Month 1 to Month 13, proving that simple averages (Phase 1) dilute recent behavioral changes. This established the mathematical necessity for a Temporal Transformer capable of native sequence modeling."

First we neglected the temporal aspect and treated each customer as a static profile. We used a simple LightGBM model to rank features by their 'Information Gain'—essentially measuring how well each feature separates defaulters from non-defaulters. This gave us a baseline ranking of which features were most predictive when treated as static snapshots.

But as we move forward towards step 2 inculcating standard deviation and other statistical measures we found that the data is not that stable and there is a lot of noise in the data. So we decided to use a more sophisticated approach to feature selection.

By including Standard Deviation in our feature pool, we found that the model prioritized temporal volatility ($B\_3\_std$) over simple averages. This confirms that credit risk is not static; it is a sequence-dependent problem. Since manual engineering found one major temporal signal ($B\_3\_std$), we are moving to a Transformer with Self-Attention to discover all other hidden temporal signals that manual aggregations might still be missin
